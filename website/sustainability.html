Feed your plants without feeding the problem

The plastic problem

The fertilizer aisle is a plastic aisle

Walk down the fertilizer section of any garden center. Plastic bottles of liquid concentrate. Plastic bags of granular fertilizer. Plastic-coated slow-release beads that remain in your soil indefinitely. Even the eco-friendly options ship in plastic containers.

Only about 9% of all plastic ever produced has been recycled. The rest sits in landfills, in oceans, and increasingly in soil.

BioPod's shell is biodegradable cellulose that dissolves in seconds and leaves zero residue. The packaging uses compostable materials. From the pod to the pouch, there is nothing to throw away and nothing left behind.

Source: OECD, Global Plastics Outlook.

The runoff problem

What you feed your plants does not stay in the pot

Approximately 2% of global greenhouse gas emissions trace to the production and application of synthetic nitrogen fertilizer. When consumers overapply synthetic fertilizer, excess nitrogen and phosphorus wash into waterways through stormwater, contributing to harmful algal blooms, hypoxic dead zones, and degraded aquatic ecosystems.

This is particularly serious in the Gulf Coast region, where nutrient runoff from residential and agricultural sources contributes to one of the largest hypoxic zones in the world.

BioPod addresses this at the source. A pre-measured dose makes over-application physically impossible. Organic, slow-release nitrogen does not carry the same runoff profile as water-soluble synthetic salts. And beneficial soil microbes help nutrients cycle within the soil rather than leaching out.

Source: Scientific Reports (Nature).

The shell story

$2,500 and three materials before we found the right one

The founder personally invested $2,500 of her own funds testing three shell material categories through hands-on bench-scale experimentation.

PVA film: rejected. Though marketed as water-soluble, PVA raises microplastic persistence concerns in soil. As an environmental scientist, the founder could not put a material in the soil that she would otherwise study as a contaminant in the waterway.

Plant-starch shells: rejected. Premature degradation in Gulf Coast humidity made shelf life unviable.

Biodegradable cellulose paper: selected. Made from FSC-sourced wood pulp fibers. Fully biodegradable, non-toxic, dissolves in 30 to 90 seconds when watered, and passes all five criteria: soil safety, moisture stability, shelf life, cost, and biodegradability.
